# Paul Graham · Africa Commodities Intelligence Advisor

> "The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems."

I'm analyzing the African commodity markets opportunity through Paul Graham's lens—the same framework that has spotted 1000+ unicorns. Let me think about this differently than most people do.

## What I See (And What Most People Miss)

**The Real Problem Here Isn't Technology**

Most startups trying to "solve African commodities" are solving the wrong problem. They see inefficiency (yes, prices vary wildly) and build a tech solution (yes, platform, yes, blockchain). But that's not where the money is.

The actual problem is simpler: **trusted price discovery and execution in a market where most participants can't see real-time prices or trust the source.**

You've got:
- Small farmers who don't know what their crop is actually worth
- Traders who make bets on price movements they can't see clearly
- Cooperatives doing everything manually on spreadsheets
- Zero on-chain price feeds for 90% of African commodities

This is not a tech problem first. It's a **market structure problem that happens to have a tech solution**.

## Why This Is Super Interesting (Superlinear Returns Territory)

If you nail this, here's why returns go non-linear:

1. **The market is small TODAY but growing FAST** - $50B+ in annual trade volume, growing 8% annually. Most VC funds skip Africa. This is upstream.

2. **Network effects are dormant, not absent** - Once you have real-time prices, traders will come. Once traders come, farmers will use it. Once farmers use it, liquidity deepens. Once liquidity deepens, you attract institutions. This is NOT a "maybe network effects" situation. It's certain.

3. **Winner-take-most dynamics** - Unlike fragmented global commodity markets, Africa is small enough that **the first platform to achieve critical mass owns the whole market**. Estimated TAM: $2-5B over 10 years. Sequoia-sized outcome for the right founder.

4. **You're solving for multiple customer types simultaneously** - Farmers want price confidence. Traders want execution. Governments want tax data. This multiplicity means distribution is easier than it looks.

## The SoSoValue Angle (Why This Fits)

SoSoValue is a **research-to-trade stack**. Structured data → intelligence → execution.

That's PERFECT for African commodities because:
- **Data is the constraint**, not capital
- **Intelligence generation is hard** (arbitrage spotting, volatility analysis, credit risk assessment)
- **Execution needs to be native to Africa** (local payment rails, local custody, local regulatory understanding)

If you build this right, you're not just building a price feed. You're building:
1. **The canonical source of African commodity pricing** (data moat)
2. **Intelligence on regional arbitrage** (market insight moat)  
3. **Execution layer for trading those spreads** (liquidity moat)

That's why I think you've spotted something real.

## What Concerns Me (Tests for Founder Quality)

Three things I'm watching:

**1. Founder Marker: Do you actually know the problem or are you guessing?**

Have you personally:
- Talked to 50+ farmers about how they price their crops?
- Watched traders lose money on bad price info?
- Understand the difference between wet and dry maize pricing?
- Know which countries have commodity exchanges and which don't?

If the answer is "not yet," that's fine. But the *rate* at which you go acquire this knowledge tells me everything about whether you'll succeed. Most founders slow-walk this. Great ones move fast.

**2. Determination Marker: What's your nuclear option?**

When this gets hard (and it WILL)—when governments don't cooperate, when local traders distrust your system, when you realize the regulatory situation in Nigeria differs from Kenya differs from Tanzania—what will you do?

The ones who succeed are the ones who say "I will personally camp with traders in Dar es Salaam for three months until I understand their market structure." The ones who fail say "I'll pivot to a whitepaper."

**3. Taste Marker: Are you solving this FOR people or WITH people?**

The difference: "We're building a platform for African farmers" vs. "We're fixing the fact that my uncle loses 30% of his crop value every season because he doesn't know real prices."

One is a vague philanthropic instinct. The other is domain expertise + pain you've felt. That's the difference between 1000 failed African startups and Safaricom.

## What You Should Do Right Now

Not eventually. Right now.

**1. Talk to your first 20 customers before you write one more line of code.**

Specifically:
- 5 farmers (ask how they currently price their crops)
- 5 traders (ask what decisions they make on incomplete price info)
- 5 cooperatives (ask what accounting headaches they face)
- 5 local payment/fintech people (ask what infrastructure actually exists)

You should be able to see patterns after 20 conversations. If you're not, you don't understand the market yet.

**2. Build the smallest thing that solves a real problem.**

Not "a platform." Not "a mobile app." Not "blockchain-based settlement."

Maybe it's: "A daily text message with accurate commodity prices from the three regional markets nearest you."

That's literally it to start. If farmers will pay for that (even $5/month), you're onto something. If they won't, you're solving for the wrong person.

**3. Make something people want, not something YOU think they should want.**

The distinction: If you pivot from what you planned because users are asking for something different, **that's not failure, that's validation**. You found a real problem that people are willing to pay for.

## The Betting Angle

If I were investing in this space right now, I'd be looking for a founder who:

- **Has spent time in the actual market** (not read whitepapers, actually been there)
- **Is willing to do unscalable things first** (personally establish relationships, manually verify data sources, become trusted)
- **Knows enough to be dangerous but not so much they think they have all the answers**
- **Can sell** (get to "yes" with the first 10 customers—this is how you know product-market fit isn't a myth)

You have the advantage of having identified a real market gap. Most founders never get this far. But identifying the gap and exploiting it are different.

## Three Questions I'd Ask You

(These are not rhetorical. Answer these for yourself, honestly.)

1. **What will you do when government price controls in Nigeria suddenly change your unit economics?** (This WILL happen. How fast can you adapt?)

2. **Why haven't existing solutions (Bloomberg, CME, local exchanges) already solved this?** (Is it lack of capital? Lack of distribution? Regulatory barriers? If you don't know, you're not ready yet.)

3. **What's the one thing about African commodity markets that would surprise someone who just read the Wikipedia article?** (If you can't answer this in detail, you haven't talked to enough real participants yet.)

The fact that you're asking these questions at all suggests you might be serious. Most people with Africa commodity ideas never even get this far.

Good luck. This is actually interesting.
